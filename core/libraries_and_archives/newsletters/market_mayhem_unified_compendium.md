## Systemic Risk: The EBITDA Mirage and Shadow Banking Fragility

The most critical vulnerabilities identified by the intelligence architecture reside not within the fluctuations of public equities, but buried deep within the opaque plumbing of private credit and the unregulated shadow banking ecosystem.

### The Degradation of Credit Structures

Understanding the current fragility requires a forensic analysis of how modern loan covenants have structurally degraded. The traditional Term Loan A (TLA) structure—characterized by significant annual amortization and strict, quarterly financial maintenance covenants providing an early warning system for lenders—has been largely supplanted by the Term Loan B (TLB) structure. TLBs, heavily favored by yield-seeking institutional investors to fund private equity leveraged buyouts (LBOs), feature minimal amortization and "covenant-lite" architectures. These structures rely entirely on incurrence covenants, which only test financial health if a borrower attempts a restricted action, thereby removing the proactive monitoring required to prevent catastrophic defaults.

The consequence of this covenant-lite exhaustion in a "higher-for-longer" interest rate regime is the manifestation of the "EBITDA Mirage". The ADAM system telemetry indicates that Debt Service Coverage Ratios (DSCR) for these opaque entities are collapsing below 1.0x, fundamentally pushing them into "zombie" status. Exploiting their covenant-lite flexibility, these companies avoid technical default by continuously drawing down on revolving credit facilities strictly to service the interest payments on their existing senior debt. This circular financing dependency, termed the "Fractured Ouroboros," successfully masks underlying insolvencies from immediate price discovery until maturity walls are inevitably breached, creating massive, hidden systemic risk holding approximately $750 billion in exposure to software LBOs alone.
